Drunk Memory

Exploring EEG Signals Using Plotly

Just how much does alcohol affect your brain waves?

A study conducted in the 1990's attempted to try to understand just how alcoholism affected brain activity in patients. In an effort to understand this behavior, subjects were hooked to an EEG to record brain wave activity. Over the course of one second, subjects were shown an image and the resulting brain waves were recorded.

In this article, we attempt to understand and visualize different patterns between the two groups to make sense of what happens to our brains through alcoholism. Although we will not attempt to explain why the subjects brains behave the way the way they do, we are curious about how we can convey how differently subjects brains behave.

What is the average voltage per test group?

As an initial step, we look at how average voltage across the brain looks over the course of the experiment. We can clearly see where the image is show to the subjects around time 60. Then, at approximately time point 120, the control subjects experience a significant deactivation of voltage as compared to their alcoholic counterparts.

What is the average voltage per subject?

Next, we break the previous plot down by subject: 10 control and 10 alcoholic. Immediately, we can see that there is a great deal of variability in the alcoholic subjects, whereas controls more consistently experience the deactivation at time point 120.

What is the average voltage per channel?

When we break these down by channel, we can see some consistent behavior between the two groups, especially around time 60, where the voltage jumps up for controls, but not for alcoholics. A subset of channels in the control subjects are highly consistently driving the deactivation that is pulling the control mean down at time point 120.

Again, what is the average voltage per channel?

This is similar to the above simulation but we can see how roughly similar the behavior is. However, the behavior for the control group seems to have wider swings that the alcoholics, possibly suggesting more brain activity. From this visualization, we can see the channels that are driving the consistent deactivation at time point 120 for the control subjects as compared to the alcoholic subjects.

Raw Electrical Activity at Electrodes

It can be helpful to watch the channel activity fluctuate through time to gain further insight into spatial relationships in the flow of electrical signals. This is a 2D projection of the locations of the nodes on the subjects head. As the animation progresses, we can see various sections of the brain light up or deactivate, though this is not consistent between the two groups.

Electrode Channel Networks

To look more closely at which channels are co-dependent, we fit a dynamic gaussian graphical model which connects two nodes in the graph if they are conditionally dependent on each other. This visualization displays which electrodes display similar voltages over each time bin, to better understand the flow of electrical signals.

Subject Network

Finally, we fit another dynamic gaussian graphical model to the data at the subject level, averaging over all channels. We might expect that alcoholic subjects are more related to alcoholics and controls to controls. This visualization shows us that at various time points we see patterns of activity that do not necessarily reflect this expectation. Red borders indicate alcoholic subjects, blue borders indicate control subjects. The color of each node is more red when there are more alcoholic connections than control connections, and more blue when there are more control connections than alcoholic connections.

Conclusions

From our visualizations, it is clear that there are some drastic differences in brain activity between the alcoholic and control groups. Overall, it seems that alcoholism depresses the magnitude of neural activity. That is, their electrical signals don't exhibit as extreme of values in response to stimuli. Someone with more expertise may be able to make sense of the data, but for now, drink responsibly.


Future Work

EEG signals are made up of multiple different types of waves, each with a different frequency. In addition to these visualizations, we would like to add some frequency analysis as well as visual representations of these differences, likely using small multiples for each channel.
